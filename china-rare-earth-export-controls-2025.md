# Did China Ban Rare-Earth Exports in 2025?

**Research question:** Did China impose a blanket rare-earth export ban in 2025, or a narrower licensing regime?

**Answer:** **No blanket ban.** On April 4, 2025, China's Ministry of Commerce (MOFCOM) and General Administration of Customs imposed export controls on specified items related to seven medium and heavy rare-earth elements: samarium, gadolinium, terbium, dysprosium, lutetium, scandium, and yttrium. Covered exports required a license. By June, MOFCOM said it had approved a number of compliant applications, and official customs data recorded 5,864.6 metric tons of aggregate rare-earth exports in May. China added separate controls later in 2025, but the official position remained that compliant exports could be licensed.

**Confidence:** High. The legal scope, licensing mechanism, approval statement, and trade figure all come from Chinese primary authorities.

**Date scope:** January 1 through December 31, 2025. Research accessed July 13, 2026.

## Bilingual evidence

### Defined scope

> 决定对下列物项实施出口管制

Faithful translation: “It was decided to impose export controls on the items listed below.”

The announcement then enumerates controlled forms, compounds, alloys, targets, magnets, and customs references associated with seven named elements. This matters: `下列物项` means the listed items, not the entire rare-earth category.

### Licensing mechanism

> 出口经营者须向国务院商务主管部门申请出口许可

Faithful translation: “Export operators must apply to the competent commerce department under the State Council for an export license.”

A license requirement can delay or deny shipments, but it is analytically different from a categorical export prohibition.

### Evidence of approvals

> 已依法批准一定数量的合规申请

Faithful translation: “A certain number of compliant applications have already been approved in accordance with law.”

MOFCOM made this statement on June 9, two months after Announcement 18 took effect. It did not disclose the number, destinations, processing time, or item mix.

### Evidence that aggregate exports continued

> 稀土，吨，5,864.6

Faithful translation: “Rare earths: 5,864.6 metric tons.”

The May customs table establishes that China's aggregate rare-earth exports did not fall to zero after April 4. It does **not** show how much of that total consisted of the seven controlled categories or traveled under newly issued licenses.

### Later official clarification

> 中国的出口管制不是禁止出口，对符合规定的申请将予以许可

Faithful translation: “China's export controls are not an export ban; applications that meet the requirements will be licensed.”

This October statement accompanied later measures and is contextual confirmation, not a substitute for reading Announcement 18's April scope.

## Timeline

- **April 4:** Announcement 18 takes effect immediately for specified items related to seven elements.
- **May:** Customs records 5,864.6 metric tons of aggregate rare-earth exports.
- **June 9:** MOFCOM says a number of compliant license applications have been approved.
- **October:** China publishes additional rare-earth controls and publicly reiterates that compliant exports can be licensed.

## What remains uncertain

- The primary sources reviewed do not disclose approval counts, denial rates, average review times, destinations, or controlled-item shipment volumes.
- Aggregate customs data must not be misrepresented as the volume exported under Announcement 18.
- “Not a blanket ban” does not mean “no commercial impact.” Licensing can create delays, uncertainty, compliance cost, and destination-specific denials.
- Later 2025 measures have distinct scopes and effective dates; they should not be collapsed into the April announcement.
